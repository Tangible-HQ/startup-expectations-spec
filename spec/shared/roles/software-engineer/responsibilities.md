# Software engineer — shared expectations

Designs, builds, and maintains software that delivers value to customers. At pre-seed this often means one generalist doing everything. By Series A the role is more specialized with clearer ownership. Read the relevant stage section before calibrating expectations.

---

## At pre-seed

The first engineer hire at pre-seed is a generalist who owns end-to-end delivery with almost no process.

**What the role looks like**
- Works directly with the founding team; no layers of management or planning process
- Covers the full stack as needed: frontend, backend, infrastructure, deployment
- Expected to ship independently within two to three weeks of joining — if they can't, the onboarding or the scope is broken
- Makes judgment calls on implementation without waiting for approval; moves fast, flags blockers immediately

**What good looks like**
- Ships working software that real users can interact with
- Raises scope and risk concerns early rather than quietly working around them
- Asks "what are we trying to learn?" before writing code, not after

**What to skip:** specialization, formal process, documentation beyond what's needed to work. The job is to build and learn fast.

---

## At seed

The seed-stage engineer has more ownership of specific systems and starts contributing to the team beyond their own output.

**Building**
- Owns one or more specific surfaces or systems end-to-end: a product area, a service, an integration
- Writes and reviews code; contributes to the team's quality bar through code review and technical feedback
- Understands the data model and API design well enough to make good local decisions

**Team contribution**
- Participates in hiring: reviews take-home exercises, conducts technical interviews, helps calibrate the bar
- Documents decisions and system behavior so the next hire doesn't have to reverse-engineer everything
- Flags technical risk and scope uncertainty in planning — not just in retrospect

**What good looks like**
- Ships features and fixes without needing constant unblocking
- Makes the team around them more effective, not just their own output
- Treats the quality bar as a team responsibility, not just a personal one

---

## At Series A

The Series A engineer operates within a larger team with defined ownership, planning processes, and cross-functional dependencies.

**Ownership and delivery**
- Has clear ownership of a system, feature area, or service with defined interfaces to other parts of the codebase
- Participates in quarterly planning: provides estimates, surfaces dependencies, flags what can't be done in the proposed timeline
- Owns reliability for their area: monitoring, on-call rotation, incident response

**Team and craft**
- Mentors more junior engineers — reviews their code, answers questions, helps them grow
- Contributes to the engineering team's practices: proposes and implements process improvements when current norms aren't working
- Writes documentation that a new engineer could use to onboard to their system without asking

**What good looks like**
- Delivers commitments predictably; escalates early when something is at risk
- Improves the systems they touch — not just feature additions, but reliability, maintainability, and clarity
- Is honest about what they don't know and asks for help rather than guessing
