# Engineering manager — shared expectations

Leads a team of engineers on delivery, quality, and people growth. The scope and depth of each responsibility shifts significantly by stage — read the relevant section before calibrating expectations.

---

## At pre-seed

This role rarely exists as a formal title. The technical co-founder or CTO covers it. If there is a first engineer hire, the founder manages them directly.

**What matters:**
- Ship fast enough to test the hypothesis; don't build process that doesn't yet have a team to run it
- Keep the technical hire unblocked and clearly scoped — one person drowning in ambiguity is worse than a slower hire

**What to skip:** formal 1:1 cadences, performance frameworks, career ladders. None of these are useful with fewer than three engineers.

---

## At seed

First formal EM hire, or a technical co-founder stepping fully into the role. The team is 3–6 engineers. The EM is part tech lead, part people manager — neither alone is enough.

**Leading people**
- Run weekly 1:1s with every direct report; surface blockers before they become delays
- Define what "good engineering" looks like on this team before the next hire interview starts — if you can't write it down, you can't hire to it
- Own onboarding: first task should be something the new hire can ship end-to-end within two weeks

**Shipping good work**
- Set and defend a quality bar appropriate to the stage — code review for correctness and security, not style
- Protect sustainable pace; a team that sprints every week will break before the Series A
- Make scope and tradeoff calls visible to founders; no silent heroics

**Working across the company**
- Represent engineering capacity honestly in planning — "we can do X or Y, not both" is a useful answer
- Coordinate directly with whoever owns product and customer conversations; don't wait for a formal process

**Hiring**
- Calibrate the interview bar before sourcing; abstract puzzles don't predict early-stage fit
- Use a work sample or short take-home that mirrors real work
- Be honest about the role — a mismatch at a 6-person company is painful for everyone

---

## At Series A

Team of 8–20+ engineers, possibly split across multiple squads. The EM may now report to a VP Engineering. The role shifts from hands-on coordination to system-building.

**Leading people**
- Run structured performance cycles: clear expectations, timely feedback, documented reviews
- Build a path for engineers to grow — even if a formal career ladder doesn't exist yet, people need to know what "leveling up" looks like
- Address underperformance early; at this scale, carrying a low performer damages team morale and trust

**Shipping good work**
- Own the team's reliability commitments — uptime targets, on-call coverage, incident response
- Drive quarterly planning: capacity, roadmap sequencing, and technical debt decisions with a business case
- Ensure practices scale with the team — what worked at 4 engineers may not work at 12

**Working across the company**
- Partner with product and design in planning, not just execution
- Communicate risk and capacity to the VP Eng or founders in terms they can act on
- Represent engineering in cross-functional conversations (sales escalations, customer commitments, compliance timelines)

**Hiring**
- Own the eng hiring pipeline: sourcing strategy, interview loops, offer calibration
- Contribute to compensation banding so offers are internally consistent and don't cause equity compression
