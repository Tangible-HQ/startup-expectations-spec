# CEO — shared expectations

Sets direction, owns the company narrative, and builds the team and resources to execute it. At pre-seed and seed the CEO is almost always a founder doing this alongside everything else. The job description changes significantly by stage — read the relevant section.

---

## At pre-seed

The CEO at pre-seed has one job: find out if the problem is real and whether this team can solve it. Everything else is secondary.

**Customer and problem**
- Talk to potential customers constantly — 3–5 conversations per week is not too many
- Own the ICP definition and the problem hypothesis; no one else can validate this for you
- Resist building until the problem is confirmed; the CEO's credibility with investors depends on what you learned, not what you shipped

**Fundraising**
- Build relationships with angels and pre-seed investors before you need money
- Know your thesis cold — why this problem, why this team, why now
- Don't optimize for valuation at pre-seed; optimize for getting to the next milestone with the right people on your cap table

**Team**
- Convince the first hires to join on limited information; this is a sales job
- Set the tone for how decisions get made and how conflict is handled — culture starts here, not at 50 people
- Protect co-founder alignment; surface tension early rather than letting it compound

**What to skip:** formal board reporting, OKRs, executive hiring, management processes. None of these are useful with fewer than five people.

---

## At seed

The CEO at seed closes the first customers personally, builds the revenue motion, and hires the people needed to scale it.

**Sales and customers**
- Own the first 5–10 closes directly — if you can't sell the product, a sales hire won't either
- Understand why each customer bought and why others didn't; this insight drives positioning and hiring
- Stay close to customer conversations even as the team grows; losing this signal is one of the most common seed-stage mistakes

**Hiring**
- Make the first non-technical hires: the first account executive, the first customer success person, the first ops hire
- Set the hiring bar explicitly — write down what "good" looks like before sourcing starts
- Every hire at this stage sets a culture precedent; choose people who reflect how you want the company to operate

**Fundraising**
- Run a tight seed process: know your metrics, have a clear ask, manage the pipeline
- Build board/investor relationships before you need them for the next raise
- Know the Series A metrics you're building toward; be able to articulate the thesis for the next round

**What to skip:** hiring a VP before the function has a repeatable motion; building process before the team is big enough to need it.

## At Series A

The CEO at Series A builds the executive team, manages the board, and makes the company legible to the outside world — investors, recruits, customers, and press.

**Executive team**
- Hire VP-level leaders in the functions that need to scale: typically sales, engineering, and product first
- Transition from doing to leading — the CEO's job is to make good people maximally effective, not to do their jobs
- Manage performance at the executive level; address misalignment early

**Board and investors**
- Set the board meeting agenda; use the board as a resource, not an audience
- Report clearly and honestly — problems surfaced early are solvable; problems hidden until they're critical are not
- Build toward the Series B thesis from day one of the Series A

**Company narrative**
- Own the external story: what the company does, why it matters, why now
- Pitch the company to recruits, press, and strategic partners consistently
- Keep the internal and external narrative aligned — employees should hear the same story as investors

**Org design**
- Design the management structure as headcount grows; don't let it happen by accident
- Preserve culture intentionally as the founding team is diluted by growth
- Set clear decision rights — what does each function own, and what requires cross-functional agreement
