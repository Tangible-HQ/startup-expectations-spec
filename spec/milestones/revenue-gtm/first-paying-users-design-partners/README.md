# First paying users or design partners

**Stage:** Seed · **Category:** Customers & revenue

> Close customers who pay real money or commit meaningful time in exchange for roadmap influence — before hiring anyone whose job depends on revenue existing.

---

{% hint style="warning" %}
**Blocking for seed.** Do not hire a sales person, a customer success manager, or a head of marketing until founders have personally closed at least 3–5 paying customers or signed design partners. If founders can't sell it, a hire won't either.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

Your first 3–5 customers who are either paying real money or have signed a design partner agreement with a time commitment and defined co-development scope. These customers prove the problem is real, the solution works at a basic level, and someone will pay for it — the three things every seed investor is trying to confirm.

## What to focus on

- **Charge from the start** — even a token amount changes the quality of feedback; customers who pay show up to calls, give honest opinions, and escalate when things break
- **Understand why each person bought** — the pattern across your first 5 closes is your go-to-market thesis; if you can't explain it, you can't replicate it
- **Understand why others didn't** — lost deals and declined trials are as valuable as closes; ask directly why they passed
- **Founders close the first deals** — this is non-negotiable; you need to understand the sales motion deeply before you can train or hire for it
- **Design partners are not free customers** — a design partner agreement should include a time commitment (regular calls, honest feedback), defined co-development scope, and a path to a paid contract

## Deprioritize for now

- Scalable sales processes — you're still learning the motion; document it as you go, but don't systematise prematurely
- Large enterprise deals — long sales cycles at this stage can consume months with no guarantee of close; prioritise speed of feedback over deal size
- Discounting to close — discounts set pricing precedents and attract customers who won't pay full price later; if someone won't pay your price, they may not be your ICP

## Paying users vs design partners

| Type | What they give you | What you give them | Right when |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|------------|
| Paying customer | Revenue, validation, honest feedback through payment behaviour | A working product | Product is usable end-to-end |
| Design partner | Time, co-development input, reference potential | Roadmap influence, early access, often discounted or free access | Product needs a specific use case to be built; you need a co-developer |

**Rule of thumb:** paying customers are better than design partners if the product already works for their use case. Design partners are better when you need to build something specific and want a committed customer to build it with.

## How to find your first customers

- Start with your network — people who trust you will give honest feedback faster than cold prospects
- Use PSV interview participants — people who described the pain vividly in discovery are warm leads
- Look for "hair on fire" customers — people actively searching for a solution right now, not just interested in the category
- Cold outbound to your ICP — a personalised email referencing a specific problem they have converts better than any marketing asset

## The first sales conversation

At this stage, your goal is not to pitch — it's to confirm the pain and make a specific ask.

1. Ask about their current situation and what's broken
2. Show the product briefly — enough to be concrete, not a full demo
3. Make a specific ask: "Would you pay $X/month to solve this problem? Can we set up a pilot?"
4. If yes, name the next step and follow up within 24 hours

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No customers** | No paying customers or design partners; pilots in progress with no commitment |
| **L1 — First pilot** | At least one customer using the product with some commitment; not yet paying |
| **L2 — First payment** | At least one customer has paid real money; feedback loop established |
| **L3 — Pattern forming** | 3–5 customers paying or in signed design partner agreements; founders can articulate why each one bought |

## How this connects to other goals

- **Problem–solution validation** — design partners often emerge from PSV interviews; the best discovery conversations end with "can I pay you to solve this?"; [PSV](../problem-solution-validation/README.md) is the prerequisite
- **Pricing and packaging** — your first customers are the test subjects for initial pricing; what they'll pay and how they want to be billed shapes the model you formalise in [pricing and packaging](../pricing-and-packaging/README.md)
- **Feedback loop and metrics** — first customers are the source of your earliest usage data; instrument before they arrive so you're capturing signal from day one
- **Strategic design partners** — a subset of design partners can become strategic — reference customers whose names open doors; identify candidates early and treat them accordingly

{% hint style="success" %}
**Done when:** At least 3 customers are paying real money or signed to a design partner agreement with a defined commitment — and you can explain in two sentences why each one said yes.
{% endhint %}
