# Pricing and packaging

**Stage:** Seed · **Category:** Customers & revenue

> Test price points, choose a value metric, and structure tiers that hold up as you grow — based on what customers actually value, not what feels round.

---

## What you are aiming for

A pricing model with a clear value metric, at least one validated price point, and a tier structure simple enough to explain in one sentence — grounded in customer willingness-to-pay data, not guesswork.

## What to focus on

- **Find your value metric first** — the thing your customers get more of as they get more value from your product (seats, usage, records, revenue processed); pricing should scale with this metric
- **Talk to customers before setting prices** — willingness-to-pay research with 5–10 customers takes half a day and saves months of wrong pricing
- **Start higher than you're comfortable with** — first-time founders almost always underprice; it's easier to lower prices than raise them; start at the high end of plausible
- **Simple beats clever** — two or three tiers with clear names and obvious differences; pricing complexity is a sales killer
- **Annual plans improve cash flow** — offer a discount for annual payment from day one; even at early stage, a year of revenue upfront changes your runway

## Deprioritize for now

- Usage-based pricing complexity before you understand usage patterns — implement it once you have 3+ months of usage data
- Enterprise custom pricing before you have enterprise customers to sell to
- Free plans — a free tier without a clear conversion path creates support burden and attracts users who will never pay

## The value metric

The value metric is what your price scales with. Getting it right matters because:
- It aligns your revenue with the value you deliver — customers pay more when they get more
- It makes pricing conversations easier — "you're paying for what you use" is simple to explain
- It makes upsell natural — growth in the customer's business drives growth in your revenue

**Common value metrics by product type:**

| Product type | Common value metrics |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Collaboration tools | Seats / users |
| Data platforms | Records, rows, or events |
| API products | API calls or compute |
| Revenue tools | Percentage of revenue processed |
| Infrastructure | Resources consumed (storage, compute) |

## Setting the price

**The van Westendorp method (5–10 customer interviews):**
Ask four questions:
1. At what price would this be so cheap you'd question the quality?
2. At what price would this be a bargain?
3. At what price would this start to feel expensive?
4. At what price would this be too expensive to consider?

The overlap between "bargain" and "starts to feel expensive" is your pricing range. Start at the high end.

**The competitor benchmark:**
- Find 3–5 alternatives your customers use or have evaluated
- Understand their pricing — public pages, sales calls, customer conversations
- Price relative to them based on where you deliver more value

## Tier structure

Most early-stage SaaS products work well with three tiers:

| Tier | Purpose | Who it's for |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Starter | Low friction entry; limits that create upgrade pressure | Small teams or individuals evaluating the product |
| Growth | The tier most paying customers will be on | Teams with a clear use case and willingness to pay |
| Enterprise | Custom pricing; white-glove onboarding; security and compliance | Organisations with procurement, SSO requirements, or custom needs |

**What creates upgrade pressure:** the limit in the lower tier should be something customers hit naturally as they get more value — not an artificial restriction that frustrates them.

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No pricing** | Free product or ad-hoc pricing; no documented model |
| **L1 — First price set** | A price exists and is being charged; no research behind it; single tier |
| **L2 — Validated price point** | Price tested with 5+ customers using willingness-to-pay research; value metric identified |
| **L3 — Tier structure working** | 2–3 tiers documented; clear upgrade triggers; annual plans available; pricing reviewed quarterly |

## How this connects to other goals

- **First paying users** — your first customers are the test subjects for pricing; what they agreed to pay is the starting data point for [first paying users](../first-paying-users-design-partners/README.md)
- **Feedback loop and metrics** — usage data from [feedback loop and metrics](../feedback-loop-metrics/README.md) tells you which features drive retention; high-value features belong in higher tiers
- **Unit economics experiments** — pricing drives your revenue per customer, which is the input to LTV in your [unit economics model](../../fundraising-runway/unit-economics-experiments/README.md)
- **Positioning and story** — pricing communicates value; tiers should reflect the positioning you've established in [positioning and story](../positioning-and-story/README.md)

**Done when:** You have a value metric, at least one validated price point backed by customer conversations, and a tier structure you can explain in one sentence — and your pricing is documented somewhere the sales or CS team can reference.
