# ICP and wedge

Most pre-seed companies fail not because they built something bad, but because they built it for everyone. "Anyone who needs X" is not a customer. This milestone forces the specificity that makes everything else — messaging, outreach, product decisions — tractable.

## What you are aiming for

A written, specific definition of your first customer and the single sharp problem you beat every alternative on. This is a hypothesis, not a commitment — but it must be specific enough to be falsifiable.

## Key concepts

**ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)** — a precise description of the type of company or person most likely to buy, get value from, and stay with your product. Not "SMBs" or "developers" — those are categories. An ICP is: "Series A SaaS companies with 10–50 engineers who are spending more than $50k/year on observability tooling and have a dedicated platform team."

**Wedge** — the single, specific problem you solve better than any alternative for your ICP. Not your full vision — the sharp entry point that gets your foot in the door. Dropbox's wedge was "sync files between my own devices reliably." Not "enterprise file collaboration." The wedge is narrow enough to win decisively.

**Why you over alternatives** — your ICP is currently solving this problem somehow: spreadsheets, a competitor, a homegrown tool, doing nothing. Your wedge must be better on a dimension they actually care about — not just features you think are important.

## How to define your ICP

Work through these dimensions until you have a specific answer for each:

| Dimension | Vague (wrong) | Specific (right) |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Who | "Small businesses" | "Ops managers at logistics companies with 20–200 drivers" |
| Industry | "B2B SaaS" | "Series A fintech companies building for SMB lending" |
| Pain | "They waste time on X" | "They manually reconcile X every Monday, taking 3–4 hours, and it blocks their weekly close" |
| Budget signal | "They'd pay for a solution" | "They're already paying $X/month for a tool that doesn't solve this" |
| Buying trigger | "Whenever they need it" | "When they cross 50 customers and the spreadsheet breaks" |
| Why you | "We're better" | "We're the only tool that does X without requiring Y" |

**A useful test:** if you can find 50 specific companies or people who match your ICP description in a day of LinkedIn or Apollo searching, it's specific enough. If you can't, it's still too broad.

## How to define your wedge

Your wedge is the one job your ICP needs done so badly that they'll switch from their current solution to try you — despite the friction of switching.

Ask yourself:
- What is the one thing our ICP is failing at that they know they're failing at?
- What does the person whose hair is on fire over this problem look like?
- What would make them say "I'll try anything" — and can we be that anything?

The wedge is not your product vision. It's the smallest valuable thing that gets a customer in the door. Everything else is roadmap.

## What "done" looks like

You should be able to complete this sentence clearly and specifically:

> "We serve [specific person/company type] who struggle with [specific problem] and currently solve it by [current alternative]. We beat that because [one specific reason]. We know this because [evidence: interviews, lost sales, etc.]."

If any part of that sentence is vague, the ICP work isn't done.

## Common gotchas

- **Too broad ICP** — "anyone who does X" means you'll build a product that's mediocre for everyone. Narrow to uncomfortable specificity, then expand after you've won that segment.
- **Wedge based on what you want to build** — the wedge should be defined by what customers actively complain about, not by what you find interesting to build.
- **Skipping "why you over alternatives"** — your ICP is comparing you to something. If you don't know what that is and why you win, your sales conversations will stall.
- **Treating ICP as permanent** — your first ICP is a hypothesis. Expect to refine it 2–3 times based on who actually converts and stays.
- **Multiple ICPs** — defining two or three ICPs simultaneously is a way of avoiding the decision. Pick one, validate it, then expand.
