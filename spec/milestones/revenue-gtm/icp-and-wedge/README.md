# ICP and wedge

**Stage:** Pre-seed · **Category:** Customers & revenue

> Define exactly who your first customer is and the one problem you beat every alternative on — before writing any code.

---

{% hint style="warning" %}
**Blocking.** Do not run discovery interviews or define MVP scope until you have a specific, falsifiable ICP hypothesis. "Anyone who needs X" is not a customer.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

A written, specific definition of your first customer type and the single sharp entry point you win on. This is a hypothesis, not a commitment — but it must be specific enough to be falsifiable and narrow enough that you can find 50 real examples of that customer in a day of searching.

## Key concepts

**ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)** — a precise description of the type of company or person most likely to buy, get value from, and stay with your product. Not "SMBs" or "developers." An ICP is: "Series A SaaS companies with 10–50 engineers spending more than $50k/year on observability tooling who have a dedicated platform team."

**Wedge** — the single specific problem you solve better than any alternative for your ICP. Not your full vision — the sharp entry point that gets your foot in the door. Narrow enough to win decisively. Everything beyond it is roadmap.

## What to focus on

- **Specificity over coverage** — narrow to uncomfortable specificity, then expand after you've won that segment; a mediocre fit for everyone is worse than a perfect fit for fifty people
- **Define "why you over alternatives"** — your ICP is already solving this problem somehow: a spreadsheet, a competitor, a homegrown tool, or doing nothing; your wedge must be better on a dimension they actually care about, not one you find interesting to build
- **Use the sentence test** — you should be able to complete the template below with no vague words; if any part is fuzzy, the work isn't done
- **Find them in a day** — if you cannot find 50 specific companies or people matching your ICP description in a day of LinkedIn or Apollo searching, it is still too broad
- **One ICP at a time** — defining two or three ICPs simultaneously is a way of avoiding the decision; pick one, validate it, then expand

## Deprioritize for now

- Building any product before this is written and tested against at least a few real customer conversations
- Defining the full addressable market — TAM comes later; nail the wedge first
- Multiple ICPs or "horizontal" positioning until the first segment is proven

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No definition** | ICP is a broad category ("SMBs", "developers"); no wedge articulated |
| **L1 — Draft hypothesis** | ICP written with some specificity; wedge identified but not yet tested against alternatives or customer language |
| **L2 — Testable** | ICP specific enough to find 50 real examples; wedge clearly differentiated from current alternatives; sentence test completable |
| **L3 — Validated** | ICP confirmed through 5+ customer conversations; wedge resonates with target customers in their own language; refined based on what you heard |

## How this connects to other goals

- **Problem–solution validation** — ICP is the input to every discovery interview; without a specific ICP, you will talk to the wrong people and get noise instead of signal; complete this first, then run [problem–solution validation](../problem-solution-validation/README.md)
- **Positioning and story** — your ICP definition and wedge are the raw material for your one-sentence positioning; don't write positioning before this is confirmed
- **Defining MVP scope** — the wedge tells you what to build first; every feature that doesn't serve the wedge is a candidate for the cut list
- **First paying users** — your ICP definition is your targeting criteria for outreach; a specific ICP means you can write a cold email that feels personal

## Team impact

The pressure to keep the ICP broad — "we don't want to limit ourselves" — is a form of avoiding the hard decision. A focused ICP is not a ceiling; it is a wedge. The companies that start narrow and win a segment expand from a position of strength. The ones that start broad rarely find a segment at all.

{% hint style="success" %}
**Done when:** You can complete this sentence clearly: "We serve [specific person or company type] who struggle with [specific problem] and currently solve it by [current alternative]. We beat that because [one specific reason]. We know this because [evidence: conversations, lost sales, or observations]." If any part is vague, the work is not done.
{% endhint %}
