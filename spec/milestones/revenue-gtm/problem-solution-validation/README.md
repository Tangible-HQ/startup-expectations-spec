# Problem–solution validation

**Stage:** Pre-seed · **Category:** Customers & revenue

> Generate evidence the problem hurts enough to pay for — before writing a line of production code.

---

{% hint style="warning" %}
**Blocking.** Do not define MVP scope or start building until this milestone is complete. Skipping it is the most expensive mistake at pre-seed.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

Enough signal from real potential customers to answer two questions with evidence, not intuition:

1. **Does the problem exist and hurt enough?** — At least 8 of 10 target interviews surface the same pain, unprompted, with concrete examples.
2. **Is your approach in the right direction?** — At least 3 people have expressed willingness to pay a specific amount, or have paid for a pilot or prototype.

Both questions must be answered. A real problem with a solution no one will pay for is not a business. A solution people love for a problem that barely hurts is not a business either.

## What to focus on

- **Problem before solution** — validate that the problem exists and hurts before testing your approach to solving it; these are sequential, not parallel
- **Talk to skeptics** — friends, warm supporters, and people who already believe in you generate false positives; seek out people who have the problem today and no reason to be kind
- **Listen, don't pitch** — a discovery interview is not a sales call; if you are talking more than the customer, you are pitching; stop and ask another open question
- **Weight evidence by cost** — a signed LOI or a paid pilot tells you more than ten enthusiastic signups; the harder it is for someone to give you a signal, the more it means
- **Pilot before product** — test the solution with the minimum fidelity needed: a Wizard of Oz experiment, a concierge service, or a clickable mockup; charge for it even if the amount is small

## Deprioritize for now

- Writing code or building infrastructure until decision criteria below are met
- Optimizing interview sample size over interview quality — 15 honest conversations beat 50 friendly ones
- Treating a waitlist or demo signups as validation evidence

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No validation** | Building on assumption; no customer conversations have happened |
| **L1 — Exploring** | 5–10 interviews completed; pain patterns emerging but not yet confirmed; no willingness-to-pay signal |
| **L2 — Problem confirmed** | 10+ interviews with consistent pain; at least 3 people expressed specific willingness to pay; solution direction identified |
| **L3 — Solution validated** | Pilot or prototype tested with target users; payment or signed commitment received; decision criteria met to begin building |

## How this connects to other goals

- **ICP and wedge** — you cannot run useful discovery interviews without a specific ICP hypothesis; complete [ICP and wedge](../icp-and-wedge/README.md) first, then use this milestone to test it
- **Defining MVP scope** — the interviews tell you what to cut; features customers didn't mention are candidates for the cut list; don't scope the MVP until you have the interview data
- **First paying users** — design partners and early customers often emerge directly from this process; the best discovery interviews end with "can I pay you to solve this now?"
- **Positioning and story** — the language customers use to describe their pain is your positioning; capture exact phrases, not paraphrases

## Team impact

The pressure to stop talking and start building is real, especially for technical founders. Treat this milestone as seriously as shipping code. A founder who has done 15 honest discovery interviews and found weak signal has saved the company months of wasted engineering. A founder who skipped this and built anyway has a harder conversation ahead.
