# Problem–solution validation

Building before validating is the most common and most expensive mistake at pre-seed. This milestone exists to generate evidence — not confidence. The goal is to find out if you're wrong as cheaply as possible, before writing a line of production code.

## What you are aiming for

Enough evidence from real potential customers that: (1) the problem hurts enough to pay for a solution, and (2) your specific approach is in the right direction. You are looking for signal, not consensus.

## The two things you are validating

**Problem validation** — does this problem exist, does it hurt, and does it hurt the person you think it hurts? You don't need a product for this. You need conversations.

**Solution validation** — given that the problem exists, is your approach to solving it something people would actually use and pay for? This requires a prototype, mockup, or pilot — not a finished product.

These are sequential. Don't validate a solution to a problem you haven't validated yet.

## How to run a discovery interview

The most common mistake is pitching instead of listening. A discovery interview is not a sales call. You are trying to understand their world, not convince them of yours.

**Who to talk to:** 10–15 people who closely match your ICP. Not friends who'll be supportive. Not people who've already said they love the idea. People who have the problem today.

**Structure:**
1. Ask about their current situation and workflow (not your product)
2. Ask about the last time the problem occurred — specifically, not hypothetically
3. Ask what they tried to solve it and why that didn't work
4. Ask what the cost of the problem is (time, money, frustration, risk)
5. Ask what a perfect solution would look like — then probe for what they'd actually pay

**Questions that generate real signal:**
- "Walk me through the last time this happened."
- "What did you do about it?"
- "How much time/money did that cost you?"
- "Have you looked for a tool to solve this? What did you find?"
- "If this were solved tomorrow, what would change for you?"

**Questions that generate false signal:**
- "Would you use a product that did X?" (hypothetical — people always say yes)
- "Do you think this is a problem?" (leading — they'll agree to be polite)
- "How much would you pay for this?" (premature — answer is meaningless without a real product)

## Evidence quality

Not all validation is equal. Weight evidence by how much it costs the person to give it.

| Signal | Strength | Why |
|--------|----------|-----|
| Paid for a pilot or prototype | Very strong | Money is a commitment, not a courtesy |
| Signed LOI or letter of intent | Strong | Written commitment, even if non-binding |
| Referred you to their colleagues unprompted | Strong | Social cost and credibility risk |
| Agreed to be a design partner with time commitment | Medium | Time is real but easier to give than money |
| Said "I'd definitely use this" in an interview | Weak | No cost; easy to say, doesn't predict behaviour |
| Signed up for a waitlist | Weak | Zero friction; tells you about interest, not intent |

## Decision criteria to proceed to building

Don't start building until you can answer yes to all three:

1. **Problem confirmed** — at least 8 of 10 target interviews surface the same pain, unprompted, with concrete examples
2. **Willingness to pay confirmed** — at least 3 people have expressed willingness to pay a specific amount, or have paid for a pilot/prototype
3. **You are the right team** — you can explain why you specifically will solve this better than someone else who tries

If you can't answer yes to all three, do more interviews. If you've done 30+ interviews and still can't, the hypothesis is probably wrong — which is the cheapest time to find that out.

## What a pilot looks like

Before code, test the solution with the minimum possible fidelity:

- **Wizard of Oz** — you manually do what the product would do, without the user knowing it's manual
- **Concierge MVP** — you deliver the outcome as a service, doing the work yourself, before automating anything
- **Prototype/mockup** — a clickable mockup that shows the workflow, used in a session with the user watching

The goal of a pilot is to test the solution hypothesis, not to impress. Charge for it — even a token amount. Money changes the quality of feedback.

## Common gotchas

- **Pitching during discovery** — if you're talking more than listening, you're pitching. Discovery interviews where you do the talking generate no useful signal.
- **Talking to supportive people** — friends, warm intros who want to help, people who already believe in you — these generate false positives. Seek out skeptics.
- **Counting interest as validation** — signups, compliments, and "I'd definitely use this" are not validation. They cost nothing to give.
- **Pivoting based on one interview** — one enthusiastic customer or one harsh critic doesn't determine the thesis. Look for patterns across 10+.
- **Validating the solution before the problem** — showing a demo before understanding the problem means you're getting feedback on your idea, not on their reality.
- **Moving to build too soon** — the pressure to stop talking and start building is real, especially for technical founders. Three interviews and a gut feeling is not enough.

## Resources

- [The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick](https://www.momtestbook.com) — the single best resource on how to run customer discovery without fooling yourself; short, practical, read it in one sitting
- [Talking to Humans — Giff Constable](https://www.talkingtohumans.com) — free book with interview scripts, templates, and a practical guide to synthesis
