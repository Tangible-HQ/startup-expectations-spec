# Company financial backbone

**Stage:** Pre-seed · **Category:** Operations

> Open a business bank account, set up bookkeeping, and establish payroll before you spend meaningful money or take on meaningful obligations.

---

{% hint style="warning" %}
**Do this in the first 30 days.** Using personal accounts for company expenses creates personal tax liability and makes bookkeeping retroactively painful. Fix it now — not after the first customer payment.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

A legal entity, a business bank account, a basic bookkeeping system, and payroll — all in place before you hire anyone, raise money, or sign a customer contract.

## What to focus on

- **Entity first** — Delaware C Corp for any venture-backed startup; everything else (banking, payroll, EIN) depends on this being done
- **Separate accounts from day one** — never commingle personal and company funds; the problems this creates are expensive and slow to fix
- **Books closed monthly** — categorise every transaction as it happens; "we'll sort it at tax time" turns a $500 problem into a $5,000 one
- **Assign ownership** — if both founders assume the other is handling finances, the books won't get done; name one person explicitly

## Deprioritize for now

- Complex financial modelling — a simple P&L and bank balance is enough at pre-seed
- Hiring a full-time finance person — a part-time bookkeeper ($200–$500/month) is sufficient until seed
- Sophisticated treasury management — a standard business bank account is fine

## Step 1 — Legal entity

For most venture-backed startups in the US, the right entity is a **Delaware C Corporation**.

| Entity type | When it fits | When it doesn't |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Delaware C Corp | Raising venture capital, issuing equity, planning exit | Sole operator, no investors, no employees |
| LLC | Simple businesses, no equity grants | Issuing stock options to employees or investors |
| S Corp | Tax efficiency for profitable small businesses | Foreign founders or investors |

**Practical steps:**
- Use [Stripe Atlas](https://stripe.com/atlas), [Clerky](https://www.clerky.com), or a startup lawyer — cost is typically $500–$2,000 all-in
- File for an EIN immediately after — free, 10 minutes at irs.gov
- Register as a foreign entity in your home state if you're incorporated in Delaware but operating elsewhere

## Step 2 — Business bank account

Open a dedicated business account immediately. Options used by most early-stage startups:

- **Mercury** — built for startups, no fees, good for teams
- **Brex** — banking plus corporate cards, popular at YC-backed companies
- **Chase Business** — traditional option if you prefer a physical bank

Get a business debit or corporate card so all company expenses are automatically separated from personal.

## Step 3 — Bookkeeping

- Use **QuickBooks Online** or **Xero** — categorise every transaction as it happens
- Reconcile your bank account monthly
- A part-time bookkeeper ($200–$500/month) is worth it once you have more than ~20 transactions per month

**What investors will ask to see:** a P&L, burn rate, and bank balance. These should be producible in under five minutes if your books are current.

## Step 4 — Payroll

If any founder is taking a salary — even a nominal one — you need a payroll provider. Paying yourself via bank transfer is a compliance problem.

- **Gusto** — most common at early-stage; handles federal and state payroll tax filings automatically
- **Rippling** — better for teams that will scale quickly
- **Deel** — if you have international contractors or employees from day one

Set up payroll before your first external hire, even if founders aren't taking salaries yet.

## Step 5 — Basic hygiene

- **Role-based email** for financial accounts (finance@yourcompany.com, not a personal address)
- **Two-factor authentication** on all financial accounts
- **Expense policy** — agree what counts as a company expense before someone buys something questionable
- **Monthly financial review** — 30 minutes to review P&L, bank balance, and burn; don't fly blind

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No setup** | No entity or personal accounts used for company expenses; no bookkeeping |
| **L1 — Entity formed** | Delaware C Corp incorporated, EIN obtained, personal and business funds still mixed |
| **L2 — Banking and books** | Business bank account open, bookkeeping running, monthly reconciliation happening |
| **L3 — Full backbone** | Entity, banking, bookkeeping, payroll, and monthly review all in place before first hire or raise |

## How this connects to other goals

- **Co-founder alignment** — salary structure and expense policy are part of the co-founder conversation; nail [co-founder alignment](../../hiring-team/co-founder-alignment/README.md) first so financial decisions are made jointly
- **Runway model and buffer** — you can't model burn accurately without clean books; bookkeeping is the input to your [runway model](../../fundraising-runway/runway-model-and-buffer/README.md)
- **Cap table hygiene** — entity formation and equity issuance are tightly linked; coordinate with [cap table hygiene](../cap-table-hygiene/README.md) from day one
- **Early financing rounds** — investors will review your financials in diligence; clean books from the start make this fast instead of painful

{% hint style="success" %}
**Done when:** You can produce a current P&L, burn rate, and bank balance in under five minutes — and every founder knows who owns the books and when they were last reconciled.
{% endhint %}
