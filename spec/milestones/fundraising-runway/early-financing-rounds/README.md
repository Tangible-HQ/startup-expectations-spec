# Early financing rounds

Raising money is a means to an end, not a milestone in itself. The goal is to buy enough time and resources to reach the next proof point — not to maximize the amount raised or the valuation. First-time founders often conflate a successful raise with a successful company. They are not the same thing.

## What you are aiming for

A closed round — with money in the bank — that gives you 12–18 months of runway to reach a specific, named milestone that justifies the next raise at better terms.

## How early financing is structured

Most pre-seed and seed rounds today use simple instruments rather than priced equity rounds. Understanding the difference matters because each has different implications for your cap table and future raises.

### SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

The most common instrument for pre-seed. Created by Y Combinator. A SAFE is not equity — it's a promise to convert into equity at a future priced round, typically at a discount or with a valuation cap.

**Key terms:**
- **Valuation cap** — the maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts. If you raise a Series A at $20M and your SAFE cap is $8M, the investor converts as if the company was worth $8M, giving them more shares.
- **Discount** — the SAFE converts at a percentage discount to the next round price (e.g., 20% discount means they pay $0.80 for every $1.00 of the next round)
- **MFN (Most Favored Nation)** — a clause in uncapped SAFEs that gives the investor the right to adopt better terms if you issue future SAFEs with better terms

**When it works:** when you need money quickly and don't want to negotiate a full term sheet; when investors are angels or small funds comfortable with the instrument.

**Watch out for:** stacking multiple SAFEs at different caps — this can create a complex conversion waterfall that surprises founders at the Series A.

### Convertible note

Similar to a SAFE but structured as debt. It accrues interest and has a maturity date by which it must convert or be repaid. Older than the SAFE and less common for new rounds, but some investors still prefer it.

**Key difference from SAFE:** it is legally debt, not equity. If the company doesn't raise before the maturity date, the noteholder can theoretically demand repayment. In practice this rarely happens, but it's a real obligation.

### Priced round

A full equity financing with a negotiated valuation, term sheet, and shareholder agreement. Standard for Seed rounds of $2M+ and almost universal at Series A.

**Key terms in a priced round:**
- **Pre-money valuation** — what the company is worth before the new money comes in
- **Post-money valuation** — pre-money + the new investment
- **Pro-rata rights** — investor's right to participate in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage
- **Board seats** — at seed, investors may request observer rights or a board seat; at Series A, a board seat is standard

## What stage maps to what instrument

| Stage | Typical instrument | Typical raise size | What it needs to unlock |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Pre-seed | SAFE (capped) | $250k–$1.5M | Proof of concept, ICP identified, team formed |
| Seed | SAFE or priced round | $1M–$4M | First paying users or clear validation signal |
| Seed extension | SAFE or bridge note | $500k–$2M | Didn't hit Series A metrics; buys time |
| Series A | Priced round | $5M–$20M | Repeatable revenue motion, team of 8–15 |

These ranges shift by market and year — they are illustrative, not rules.

## How to size the round

Raise the amount that gets you to your next fundable milestone with a 20–30% buffer. Not more — unnecessary dilution is real. Not less — running out of money 3 months before the milestone is a crisis.

**Work backwards:**
1. What is the one milestone that makes the next raise at better terms possible? (e.g., $30k MRR, first enterprise contract, 1,000 active users)
2. What does it cost per month to operate at the team size needed to reach it?
3. How many months will it realistically take?
4. Multiply monthly burn × months × 1.25–1.3 buffer = target raise amount

**A common mistake:** raising $500k when you need $1.2M to reach the milestone. You'll run out of money in the middle of your story, which is the worst time to raise again.

## Use of funds

Every investor will ask: "What will you do with this money?" You need a specific answer, not a vague one.

**Strong answer:** "We'll use $800k over 14 months — $400k on engineering (2 hires), $200k on customer acquisition experiments, and $200k on operations — to get from $8k to $50k MRR, which we believe unlocks a Series A conversation."

**Weak answer:** "Product development, sales, and marketing."

The use-of-funds story is also a signal of operational maturity. If you can't explain where the money goes, investors assume you don't know how to run the company.

## Who to raise from

At pre-seed, most money comes from angels and small pre-seed funds. The right investors add more than money — they add introductions, customer relationships, or recruiting leverage.

| Source | What they offer | Watch out for |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Angels (individuals) | Fast decisions, flexible terms, often domain expertise | No institutional support; harder to get follow-on help |
| Pre-seed funds ($5–30M funds) | Some infrastructure, portfolio network | May have pro-rata expectations that complicate your seed |
| Accelerators (YC, Techstars) | Network, credibility, structured program | Equity cost (YC takes 7%); time commitment of the program |
| Friends and family | Fastest and least conditional | Can damage relationships; no value-add beyond capital |
| Strategic investors (corporates) | Market validation, customer relationships | Slow, complex terms, potential conflicts with future investors |

**A note on accelerators:** YC in particular changes the fundraising calculus significantly — the network, signal, and demo day audience are worth the dilution for most early-stage companies. Apply if you're pre-product and pre-revenue.

## Common gotchas

- **Raising too early** — investors fund milestones, not ideas. Raising before you have a validated problem or an ICP means you're selling a thesis, not evidence, and you'll either not close or raise at worse terms.
- **Optimizing for valuation over terms** — a high cap SAFE from an investor who adds nothing is worse than a lower cap from someone who opens 10 doors. Valuation is one variable; investor quality matters more at this stage.
- **Stacking too many SAFEs** — three or four SAFEs at different caps creates a conversion waterfall that can surprise you at the Series A and upset new investors.
- **No clear milestone for the round** — "we'll use it to grow" is not a milestone. If you can't name what this round is designed to prove, you're raising without a plan.
- **Taking strategic money without thinking it through** — a corporate investor who owns 10% of your pre-seed can make future VCs nervous. Understand the implications before closing.
- **Spending before the wire clears** — verbal commitments and signed SAFEs are not money. Do not hire or commit to expenses until funds are in your bank account.
