# Remote and global hiring

**Stage:** Series A · **Category:** Hiring & team · **[conditional]**

> Build a hiring process and compensation philosophy that works across geographies — before a key hire falls through because you can't pay them legally.

---

{% hint style="info" %}
**Conditional.** This milestone applies when you're actively hiring or managing team members outside your home country, or when your hiring pipeline consistently surfaces strong candidates in other geographies.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

A repeatable, legally compliant way to hire and pay people in at least two geographies — without setting up a foreign entity until you're sure the market warrants it. The goal is that a great candidate in a different country is a solvable ops problem, not a reason to pass on them.

## What to focus on

- **Employer of Record (EOR) before entity** — use an EOR like Deel, Remote, or Rippling to employ workers abroad without establishing a local legal entity; the EOR is the employer of record and handles local payroll, taxes, and benefits
- **Contractor vs employee — get this right** — misclassifying an employee as a contractor creates tax liability and potential back-pay claims; most countries have clear tests for this; when in doubt, use an EOR
- **Location-based compensation bands** — decide your compensation philosophy before offers start going out: will you pay San Francisco rates globally (expensive, simple), local market rates (complex, cheaper), or something in between?
- **Benefits parity at minimum required levels** — every country has statutory minimums (parental leave, vacation, health coverage in some markets); your EOR will handle the legal floor, but you'll need a view on what you offer above it
- **Time zone overlap is a hiring criterion** — a team spanning 14 time zones with no overlap is a coordination problem that compounds over time; name your required overlap window before you hire

## Deprioritize for now

- Setting up a foreign subsidiary for your first hire in a country — an EOR is cheaper and faster until you have 5+ people in a market
- Harmonizing every benefit globally — local statutory requirements vary so much that full parity is usually impractical and expensive
- Running your own international payroll — the complexity and compliance risk of self-managing cross-border payroll isn't worth it below 20+ international employees

## EOR vs entity: when to make the switch

An Employer of Record is the right structure when:
- You have 1–3 employees in a country
- You're not sure if the market or role is permanent
- You don't have local HR, legal, or finance infrastructure

A local entity makes sense when:
- You have 5+ employees in a country (EOR fees become significant)
- You need a local presence to sell, bill, or operate (not just to hire)
- The employment relationship requires local employer branding (Germany, France)

Most Series A companies never need to open a foreign entity for purely hiring-related reasons.

## Compensation philosophy

Before the first cross-border offer, agree on a compensation philosophy:

| Approach | How it works | Trade-off |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Single global band** | Pay one rate regardless of location (usually US/SF) | Simple; expensive; can overpay in some markets |
| **Location-adjusted bands** | Pay to local market; adjust band by city/country tier | Fair to local markets; complex; requires benchmarking by geo |
| **Zone-based bands** | Group countries into 2–4 tiers with a multiplier | Pragmatic middle ground; common at Series A |

Whichever you choose, document it before the first offer. Inconsistent comp philosophy becomes a retention and equity problem fast.

## Common countries and their quirks

| Country | Key consideration |
|---------|------------------|
| **UK** | Statutory employer NI contributions (~13%); IR35 rules for contractors are strict |
| **Canada** | Provincial variation in employment law; Quebec is materially different |
| **Germany** | Strong employee protections; termination is expensive and slow; works councils if >5 employees |
| **India** | Contractor classification is common but scrutinised; EOR works well here |
| **Brazil** | One of the most complex employment regimes globally; don't DIY payroll |
| **Australia** | Superannuation (employer pension contribution ~11%) is mandatory |

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No process** | Paying international team members via PayPal or wire; no legal structure; compliance risk unmanaged |
| **L1 — EOR in place** | Using an EOR for at least one country; contracts and payroll handled correctly |
| **L2 — Comp philosophy defined** | Location-based comp bands documented; benefits parity at statutory minimum; equity handling defined for non-US employees |
| **L3 — Repeatable global hiring** | Can hire in any EOR-supported country within 2 weeks; comp bands updated annually; local statutory requirements reviewed per-country |
