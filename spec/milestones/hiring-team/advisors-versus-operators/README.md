# Advisors versus operators

**Stage:** Series A · **Category:** Hiring & team

> An advisor with great credentials and light engagement is not a substitute for an operator with ownership and full attention — and conflating the two wastes equity and time.

---

## What you are aiming for

A clear separation between people who give advice (advisors) and people who own outcomes (operators). Advisors have defined scope, calibrated equity, and time commitments they've actually agreed to. Operators have a role, a charter, and accountability for results. Neither is confused about which one they are.

## What to focus on

- **Define what you need before you give equity** — "we should get an advisor in this space" is not a good enough reason; name the specific question you need answered and whether an advisor can realistically answer it better than a $500 strategy call
- **Advisors advise; they don't decide** — an advisor who starts making decisions, running conversations with customers, or directing employees has become an operator without the accountability that comes with it; notice when this happens
- **Calibrate equity to actual time and leverage** — advisor grants in the 0.1–0.5% range (on a 2-year vest, no cliff) are standard; above that, you're either paying for seniority you should hire directly or letting someone negotiate equity without delivering value
- **Operators need full ownership** — a person who is nominally VP of X but hedges, routes decisions to founders, and treats the role as advisory is a senior advisor in VP clothing; this is the most common and expensive mistake
- **Time commitments must be explicit** — an advisor who agrees to "occasional calls" will have a different definition of "occasional" than you do; name the expected hours per month before the grant

## Deprioritize for now

- Accumulating advisors as social proof — a long list of advisor names on your website signals that you couldn't hire operators
- High-profile advisors who are spread across 20 companies — they don't have enough attention to give you meaningful input
- Giving advisors board observer seats by default — that's a board question, not an advisor question

## The FAST agreement

Use the [Founder / Advisor Standard Template (FAST)](https://fi.co/fast) from the Founder Institute. It's the de facto standard for advisor agreements. It covers:

- **Service description** — what the advisor actually does; be specific
- **Monthly time commitment** — number of hours expected
- **Equity grant** — percentage, vesting schedule, acceleration
- **Exclusivity and conflict clauses** — whether the advisor can advise competitors

Two-year vesting with no cliff is standard for advisors. The vesting schedule reflects that advisor relationships are short-to-medium term, not decade-long like employee grants.

## Advisor equity benchmarks

| Stage | Typical grant (% fully diluted) | Notes |
|-------|--------------------------------|-------|
| Pre-seed | 0.25–0.5% | Only give this to someone providing genuine early-stage leverage |
| Seed | 0.1–0.25% | Standard for a domain expert or functional advisor with monthly commitment |
| Series A+ | 0.05–0.1% | Appropriate for senior advisors; above this, consider hiring directly |

Never grant advisor equity without a vesting schedule. Unvested advisor equity is a cap table problem.

## Types of advisors and what they actually deliver

| Type | What they can do | What they can't do |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Domain expert** | Introductions, regulatory navigation, customer credibility | Build the product or run the sales motion |
| **Functional advisor** (ex-VP of Sales, ex-CTO) | Playbook guidance, hiring calibration, process review | Own a function or be accountable for results |
| **Investor-as-advisor** | Deal flow, investor intros, board-level pattern recognition | Replace an operator in a functional role |
| **Customer advisor** | Use-case validation, reference calls, early feedback | Represent the broader market |

## When to hire an operator instead

You need an operator, not an advisor, when:

- A function doesn't have anyone accountable for it and the function matters (marketing, sales, finance)
- You're making the same recurring mistakes and need someone to own the fix
- The work requires more than 10 hours per month of real time from the person
- The decision-making can't come from you but needs to come from someone

Advisors work well for narrow, high-leverage questions. They don't work as substitutes for headcount.

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — Undefined** | Advisor relationships informal; no agreements; equity given without vesting or clear scope |
| **L1 — Agreements in place** | FAST agreements signed for all advisors; scope and time commitments written down |
| **L2 — Active management** | Advisors are actually delivering; quarterly check-in on whether the relationship is still useful; equity calibrated to contribution |
| **L3 — Clear separation** | Every function that matters has an operator who owns it; advisors supplement not substitute; no advisor has drifted into operator territory without title or accountability |

## How this connects to other goals

- **Early team hires** — if you're reaching for advisors to fill a functional gap, the question is whether you should be hiring an operator instead; [early team hires](../early-team-hires/README.md) is where that decision lives
- **Founding roles and equity** — advisor equity grants affect the cap table the same way as employee grants; make sure advisors are modelled in your option pool usage
- **Operating rhythm** — advisors work best when they engage with your operating cadence (e.g. a monthly call timed with your own reviews); [operating rhythm](../operating-rhythm/README.md) is the mechanism
