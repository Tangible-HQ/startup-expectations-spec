# Immigration and work authorization

**Stage:** Series A · **Category:** Hiring & team · **[conditional]**

> Know the visa path for a key hire before you start the interview process — not after you've made an offer and the candidate is waiting.

---

{% hint style="info" %}
**Conditional.** This milestone applies when you want to hire someone who doesn't already have the right to work in your country, or when a key team member's work authorization depends on their employer sponsorship and you need to maintain or transfer it.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

A clear-eyed view of which visa paths are available for the roles you're hiring, what the timelines and costs are, and what your backup plan is if the primary path fails. Not expertise — that requires an immigration attorney — but enough fluency to make good hiring decisions and not lose candidates to a process you didn't anticipate.

## What to focus on

- **Talk to an immigration attorney before making the offer** — not after; a 30-minute call with a specialist will tell you whether a candidate's path is straightforward, complicated, or effectively closed; this call costs $300–500 and saves weeks of uncertainty
- **Timeline is the most underestimated variable** — H-1B petitions take months; green card processes take years; build the timeline into your hiring plan, not your onboarding plan
- **The company is the sponsor, and sponsorship has obligations** — if the employee leaves or is let go, their visa status may be immediately affected; understand your obligations before you assume them
- **EOR and remote work as an alternative** — if a candidate lives abroad and a sponsorship path is complex or uncertain, hiring them via an Employer of Record where they live is often faster, cheaper, and less risky than relocating them
- **Have a backup plan for every key hire** — if your CTO candidate's O-1 petition takes longer than expected, what is the interim plan? Remote from their current country? Extended contractor engagement?

## Deprioritize for now

- Sponsoring multiple hires simultaneously without an immigration law firm on retainer — the administrative load compounds fast
- Green card (permanent residence) sponsorship for new hires — this is appropriate for existing employees you're committed to, not candidates whose fit is unproven
- Building an in-house immigration program — use outside counsel until you have a dedicated HR or legal team

## Common US visa categories for startup hires

### H-1B — Specialty occupation

The most common path for professional roles (engineering, finance, marketing).

- **How it works:** Employer files a petition; employee works in a "specialty occupation" requiring a bachelor's degree or equivalent
- **The catch:** H-1B is subject to an annual lottery (cap of ~85,000 visas per year); odds of selection in a given year are roughly 30–60% depending on the cap-subject pool
- **Timeline:** File in April; lottery in March; earliest start date October 1 of the same year; if not selected, must try again next year
- **Cost to employer:** $3,000–$8,000 in filing fees + attorney fees ($3,000–$6,000); total ~$6,000–$14,000
- **Cap-exempt employers:** Universities, nonprofit research institutions, and their affiliates are cap-exempt; H-1B can start any time

### O-1A — Extraordinary ability

Strong path for senior hires with a documented track record of distinction.

- **How it works:** Requires evidence of extraordinary ability: awards, published work, high salary relative to peers, critical role at distinguished organisations, membership in exclusive groups
- **The advantage:** No lottery; can be filed any time; strong approval rate when well-documented
- **Timeline:** Premium processing available (15 business days); standard processing 3–4 months
- **Cost:** $4,000–$10,000 in attorney fees + filing fees; higher than H-1B but no lottery risk

### TN — Trade NAFTA/USMCA (Canada and Mexico only)

Fastest path for Canadian and Mexican citizens in specific professional roles.

- **How it works:** Admitted at the border or port of entry; no petition required for most roles; applies to ~60 qualifying professions (engineer, scientist, accountant, lawyer, etc.)
- **Timeline:** Hours to days; no waiting period
- **Cost:** ~$50 USCIS fee + minimal attorney involvement
- **Limitation:** Employer-tied; must change status if changing employers; not a path to permanent residence

### E-3 — Australian nationals

Similar to H-1B for Australian citizens; 10,500 visas per year (rarely hits the cap).

- **How it works:** Employer files a Labor Condition Application; Australian national applies at a US consulate
- **Timeline:** 2–6 weeks from consulate appointment
- **Cost:** Low relative to H-1B; ~$1,000–$3,000 total

### L-1 — Intracompany transferee

Relevant if you're expanding internationally and want to move a foreign-office employee to the US.

- **How it works:** Transfers an employee from a foreign affiliate to the US entity; requires at least 1 year of employment abroad in the prior 3 years
- **L-1A** for managers/executives; **L-1B** for specialized knowledge workers
- **Timeline:** Premium processing available; standard 3–4 months
- **Advantage:** Direct path to EB-1C green card (for managers and executives)

## Green cards — when to start

Green card sponsorship is a multi-year commitment. Common paths:

- **EB-1A (extraordinary ability):** Self-petition; no employer sponsor required; 1–2 years
- **EB-1C (multinational executive/manager):** Requires L-1A status first; 1–3 years
- **EB-2/EB-3 (PERM labor certification):** Most common; requires employer to demonstrate no qualified US worker is available; 3–8+ years for Indian and Chinese nationals due to visa backlogs

Don't start green card sponsorship as a retention tool unless you're prepared to see it through; stopping mid-process can create legal and relational complications.

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — Reactive** | Immigration issues surface during or after offers; no process; decisions made without legal advice |
| **L1 — Attorney relationship** | Immigration attorney on call; basic understanding of which visa paths apply to your hiring profile |
| **L2 — Proactive planning** | Visa path and timeline assessed before roles are posted for candidates who may require sponsorship; costs budgeted |
| **L3 — Program in place** | Immigration tracking for all sponsored employees; renewal timelines on calendar; transitions between visa types managed proactively |

## How this connects to other goals

- **Remote and global hiring** — [remote and global hiring](../remote-and-global-hiring/README.md) is the alternative when relocation or sponsorship isn't the right path; the two milestones are complementary
- **Early team hires** — immigration timelines should factor into hiring sequencing; a key engineering hire with a 6-month visa timeline needs to be in your plan earlier than a local hire
- **Runway model and buffer** — immigration costs ($6,000–$14,000 per sponsored hire) are a real budget line; include them in your hiring cost model
