# Operating rhythm

**Stage:** Seed · **Category:** Hiring & team

> A lightweight operating cadence — weekly priorities, brief standups, and honest retrospectives — keeps a small team aligned without creating the meeting overhead that kills focus.

---

## What you are aiming for

A set of recurring rituals that keep the team pointing in the same direction, surface blockers early, and close the feedback loop between what you planned and what you shipped — without replacing maker time with meeting time.

## What to focus on

- **Weekly written priorities, not just daily standups** — the most useful coordination tool at seed stage is a shared weekly priority doc; "what are you working on this week and what would stop you?" is more useful than a daily async standup
- **Differentiate coordination from decision-making** — status updates don't need a meeting; decisions do; only schedule meetings that require real-time judgment
- **Set goals quarterly, review weekly** — goals at seed are 6–12 week sprints, not annual plans; the review cadence should be fast enough to catch drift before it compounds
- **Retrospectives are how you improve the process** — a 45-minute retro every 4–6 weeks where the team honestly says "what slowed us down" is worth more than any process improvement framework
- **Protect deep work time** — designate no-meeting days or blocks; meetings that can be async should be async; maker time is a resource that gets spent permanently

## Deprioritize for now

- OKRs with elaborate scoring systems — at 5 people, the overhead of OKR software is higher than the benefit
- Daily all-hands meetings — too frequent; use async for status
- Annual planning cycles — too slow; plan quarterly at most, review monthly

## The minimum viable operating rhythm

**Weekly (30 min, synchronous):**
- What did we each ship or close last week?
- What are the top 3 priorities this week?
- What is blocked? Who needs to unblock it?
- Any decisions that need to be made as a team this week?

**Written (async, posted Monday):**
- Each person posts their weekly priorities in a shared doc or Slack channel
- Founders post company-level priority for the week

**Monthly (60 min):**
- Progress toward quarterly milestones: are we on track?
- Anything that should change about our priorities?
- Team health: energy, morale, any emerging friction?

**Quarterly (half day):**
- What did we set out to do? What did we actually do?
- What are the 3–5 most important things for the next quarter?
- Is the team the right size and shape for what's ahead?

## Goal-setting at seed stage

At seed, goals should be:

- **Short time horizon** — 6–12 weeks maximum; the business changes too fast for longer cycles
- **Outcome-oriented, not activity-oriented** — "close 5 paying customers" beats "run 20 sales calls"
- **Few in number** — 3–5 goals maximum per quarter; more is a wish list, not a plan
- **Honest about what matters** — the top goal should be the one where failure would mean the company is in trouble; not the one that looks good in a board update

Track goals in a shared doc, not in your head. Review weekly.

## Meeting hygiene

Every meeting should have:
- A written agenda posted in advance
- A clear decision or output (if no output is possible, it should be async)
- A note-taker and written summary posted within an hour

Every meeting should not have:
- More people than necessary to make the decision
- A recurring slot that outlived its usefulness

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — Ad hoc** | No recurring cadence; coordination happens informally; priorities unclear across the team |
| **L1 — Weekly sync** | Weekly team sync running; blockers surfaced; founders aligned on top priority |
| **L2 — Written priorities** | Weekly written priorities shared; quarterly goals set; retros running |
| **L3 — Feedback loop closed** | Goals tracked weekly; retros improving the process; meeting time is protected maker time; the team can articulate what they're working on and why without asking a founder |
