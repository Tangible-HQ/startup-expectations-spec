# Co-founder alignment

The most common reason early startups fail isn't the market or the product — it's co-founder conflict. Most of it is preventable if you have the right conversations before you're under pressure, not during.

## What you are aiming for

A written agreement covering the scenarios that will stress your partnership, completed before you raise money or hire anyone. Not a legal formality — a forcing function for honest conversation while stakes are still low.

## The conversations to have now

Don't skip these because they feel premature. Every one of them becomes harder and more expensive to resolve after the company has value.

| Topic | What to decide |
|-------|----------------|
| Decision authority | Who has final say on product direction, key hires, fundraising terms, and pivots? |
| Departure terms | What happens to equity if someone leaves in month 6? Month 18? Is it different if they're fired vs. resign? |
| Salary | What do founders get paid now, and what triggers a raise? |
| Early exit | If you get a $3M acqui-hire offer in 18 months, what's the decision process? |
| Outside commitments | Is everyone full-time? If not, when does that change, and what's the trigger? |
| Tie-breaking | If you deadlock on a major decision, how do you resolve it? |

## Key concepts

**Vesting** — your equity is earned over time, not granted upfront. Without vesting, a co-founder who leaves after six months walks away with a permanent, undilutable stake. Standard terms are:

- 4-year total vesting period
- 1-year cliff — nothing if you leave before month 12
- Monthly vesting after the cliff (1/48th per month)

If you incorporated without vesting, fix it before any investor sees your cap table.

**Good-leaver vs. bad-leaver** — when a founder departs, the company typically has the right to buy back unvested shares at cost. "Good leaver" provisions (health, family) may allow more generous treatment than "bad leaver" (fired for cause, competing against the company).

**IP assignment** — any code, designs, or inventions built before incorporation must be formally assigned to the company via an IP assignment agreement. If this is missing, investors will flag it in diligence.

## What to formalize

A Founder Agreement (or Co-Founder Agreement) should cover:

- Roles and decision ownership per founder
- Equity split and vesting schedule
- IP assignment to the company
- Departure terms and buyback rights
- Salary structure and how it changes
- What constitutes a "major decision" requiring joint agreement

Most startup lawyers offer flat-fee packages (typically $1,500–$3,000) for this. Do it once, do it properly.

## Common gotchas

- **"We trust each other"** — trust doesn't prevent different memories of what was agreed. The conversation is hardest before you need it and nearly impossible after conflict starts.
- **Verbal-only agreements** — memories diverge under stress. Write it down.
- **50/50 with no tie-breaker** — equal splits work fine until they don't. Agree on who has the casting vote before you're deadlocked.
- **No vesting on founder shares** — this is the single most common cap table problem that kills early funding conversations.
- **Waiting until after the raise** — investors will ask about co-founder agreements. Not having one signals you're avoiding the hard conversation.
- **Skipping the exit scenario** — "we'll cross that bridge when we get there" is how co-founders end up in litigation over a small acquisition offer.
