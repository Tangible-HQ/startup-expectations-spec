# Founding roles and equity

How you split equity and define roles in the first week has consequences that follow the company for a decade. Unlike most early decisions, this one is very hard to undo cleanly.

## What you are aiming for

A cap table and roles setup that's clean for investors, fair given expected future contribution, and specific enough that everyone knows who owns which decisions day-to-day.

## Equity splits

There's no universally correct number. What matters is that the split reflects future contribution (not past ideas), is documented, and has vesting. Arguing about the right split is time well spent. Defaulting to 50/50 because the conversation is uncomfortable is not.

| Split | When it fits | Watch out for |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Equal (e.g. 50/50) | Both founders are full-time, complementary skills, equal commitment | Deadlock risk without a clear tie-breaker mechanism |
| Unequal (e.g. 60/40) | One founder is driving the idea, has more risk, or brings existing IP | Can create resentment if the minority founder's contribution grows and the split doesn't feel fair later |
| Three or more founders | Full skill coverage without early hires | Thin individual stakes; voting complexity on key decisions |

Factors that legitimately justify unequal splits: who had the original idea and built early IP, who is taking a larger salary cut, who has the domain expertise the company is built around, who is likely to be CEO long-term.

## Vesting — non-negotiable

Every founder must vest. This is not optional if you plan to raise from investors.

**Standard:** 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff, monthly vesting thereafter.

- **Cliff** — if you leave before month 12, you receive no equity
- **Monthly vesting** — after month 12, you earn 1/48th of your grant per month
- **Acceleration** — some agreements include single- or double-trigger acceleration on acquisition; agree on this upfront

If you incorporated without vesting, a "reverse vesting" agreement can apply vesting retroactively. Do this before investors see your cap table — it's a standard fix that most lawyers can execute quickly.

## Roles at pre-seed

At this stage, roles are about decision ownership, not org-chart status. Two founders with five VP titles each is a red flag to investors.

| Role | What it actually means at pre-seed |
|------|-------------------------------------|
| CEO | External face, final call on fundraising terms and key hires, accountable to any board or advisors |
| CTO | Technical direction, architecture decisions, engineering quality bar — often the only engineer |
| CPO | Product scope and prioritization — almost always the same person as CEO at this stage |

Keep titles to 2–3 maximum. No VP, no C-suite beyond CEO/CTO until you have people reporting to those roles.

## Decision rights

Agree upfront — in writing — on which decisions each founder owns independently and which require joint agreement.

**Solo decisions (within your function):**
Engineering stack, sprint priorities, customer scheduling, copy changes, vendor selection under a threshold

**Joint decisions (require both founders):**
Fundraising terms, major pivots, equity grants to anyone, executive hires, acquisition discussions, changes to founder compensation

The goal is to avoid every decision needing a meeting while ensuring high-stakes choices get genuine input from both sides.

## Common gotchas

- **No founder vesting** — the single most common cap table problem that kills early funding conversations. Fix this on day one.
- **Equity for past work** — vesting equity should reflect future contribution, not credit for the original idea or early nights. Compensating past work with equity usually creates resentment when new team members join.
- **Title inflation** — "VP of Product" at two people signals inexperience to investors and creates expectation debt with future employees who report to that title.
- **Defaulting to 50/50 to avoid conflict** — the difficult conversation about split now is far cheaper than resentment about it later, especially if one founder's contribution changes.
- **Missing IP assignment** — any code, designs, or inventions built before incorporation must be formally assigned to the company. Investors will surface this in diligence.
- **Forgetting about option pool** — investors will typically require an unallocated option pool (usually 10–20%) before or as part of your seed round. Factor this into your cap table model now.

## Resources

- [The Founder's Dilemmas — Noam Wasserman](https://www.amazon.com/dp/0691158304) — the definitive research-based book on how early equity and role decisions predict startup outcomes; read chapters 2–4 before any equity conversation
- [Slicing Pie — Mike Moyer](https://slicingpie.com) — a framework for dynamic equity splits based on actual contribution, useful if fixed splits feel premature or unfair given different start dates
- [Carta: Cap Table 101](https://carta.com/learn/equity/cap-table/) — practical guide to understanding what a cap table is, what goes on it, and how it changes through funding rounds
- [Paul Graham: The Equity Equation](http://paulgraham.com/equity.html) — short essay on the logic of giving up equity; useful framing before negotiating any split or grant
- [Y Combinator on equity splits](https://www.startupschool.org) — search "equity split" in YC Startup School; Dalton Caldwell's talks are particularly direct on what investors think when they see your cap table
