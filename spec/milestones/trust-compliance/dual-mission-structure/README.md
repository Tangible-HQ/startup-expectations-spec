# Dual mission or structure

**Stage:** Series A · **Category:** Trust & compliance · **[conditional]**

> A B Corp or nonprofit arm can reinforce your mission and credibility — but only if you're willing to take on the governance overhead it actually requires. Don't structure for optics.

---

{% hint style="info" %}
**Conditional.** This milestone applies only when your mission — environmental impact, social equity, public benefit — is a core part of your product and go-to-market, not a brand positioning choice. If your mission is primarily a narrative rather than an operating constraint, the costs of formal dual-structure outweigh the benefits.
{% endhint %}

## What you are aiming for

A legal and governance structure that makes your mission-driven commitments durable, credible, and resistant to short-term commercial pressure — without creating reporting and governance overhead that distracts from building the company. The right structure depends on what your mission is, how it intersects with your revenue model, and what you're willing to trade off.

## What to focus on

- **Decide why you're doing this before choosing the structure** — are you trying to protect the mission from future board or shareholder pressure? signal credibility to customers or donors? access grant funding? meet procurement requirements? each reason points to a different structure
- **Understand the governance obligations before you commit** — B Corp certification requires recertification every three years with significant documentation; a nonprofit arm requires separate governance, separate financials, and a board; these are real operational costs, not marketing decisions
- **B Corp certification and PBC incorporation are different things** — you can be a certified B Corp without being a Public Benefit Corporation, and vice versa; most companies that pursue the B Corp brand do certification only, which doesn't change your legal structure
- **A nonprofit arm is a separate legal entity** — if you create a 501(c)(3) or equivalent, it has its own board, its own financial reporting obligations, its own fundraising, and its own governance; the commercial entity and the nonprofit cannot simply share resources without documented agreements

## Deprioritize for now

- Pursuing B Corp certification as a marketing decision — the certification process takes 6–12 months and costs $1,000–$50,000 depending on company size; buyers and investors increasingly scrutinise whether it's substantive
- Creating a nonprofit arm before you've proven the commercial model — a nonprofit arm that has no activities is a compliance cost without a benefit
- Climate certifications that require supply chain data you don't have — plan for what you can actually measure and verify

## Common structures

### Certified B Corporation

**What it is:** A certification from B Lab (nonprofit) that your company meets verified standards of social and environmental performance, accountability, and transparency. Not a legal structure — you remain a standard corporation.

**What it requires:**
- Pass the B Impact Assessment (BIA) — a 200-question assessment across governance, workers, community, environment, and customers; minimum score of 80/200
- Sign the B Corp Declaration of Interdependence
- Amend your legal documents to consider stakeholders, not just shareholders (varies by state)
- Recertify every 3 years with updated BIA scores

**Costs:** $2,000–$50,000 in annual certification fees depending on revenue; internal time to prepare the BIA and document policies is typically 40–100 hours

**Best for:** Companies that want external validation of existing practices; B2B companies selling to enterprises with ESG procurement requirements; consumer brands where mission is a differentiator

### Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)

**What it is:** A legal form of for-profit corporation (available in Delaware and most US states) that explicitly includes a public benefit purpose in its charter alongside shareholder returns.

**What it requires:**
- Incorporate as a PBC from the start, or convert from a C corp (requires board and shareholder approval)
- State a specific public benefit purpose in the certificate of incorporation
- Consider stakeholder interests in decisions (directors have a higher standard of care)
- Report biennially to shareholders on public benefit performance (Delaware PBC)

**Costs:** Same as incorporating a standard C corp; annual compliance similar; no external certification required

**Best for:** Companies where mission-driven decision-making must be legally protected from shareholder pressure; founders who want structural protection for the mission without ongoing certification overhead

### Nonprofit arm (501(c)(3) or equivalent)

**What it is:** A separate legal entity with tax-exempt status that pursues charitable or educational purposes alongside the commercial entity.

**What it requires:**
- Incorporate separately
- Apply for and maintain tax-exempt status (IRS Form 1023; 4–6 month process)
- Separate board of directors with its own governance
- Annual financial reporting (Form 990)
- Restrictions on commercial benefit from the nonprofit; documented arm's-length transactions between the for-profit and nonprofit
- Separate fundraising, grant management, and accounting

**Costs:** $2,000–$5,000 for legal setup; ongoing compliance adds 20–40 hours/year minimum

**Best for:** Companies with a genuine charitable or educational programme separate from the commercial product; companies that want to access grant funding or tax-deductible donations

### Climate and carbon reporting

**What it is:** Voluntary reporting on carbon emissions, reduction targets, and progress — through frameworks like GHG Protocol, CDP, or SBTi (Science Based Targets initiative).

**What it requires:**
- Scope 1 and 2 emissions measurement (your direct emissions and energy use); increasingly, Scope 3 (supply chain and product use)
- A quantified reduction target and a credible plan to meet it
- Annual public disclosure

**Costs:** $5,000–$50,000/year for emissions measurement and reporting, depending on complexity

**Best for:** Companies with material environmental footprint; companies selling to large enterprises with their own carbon commitments; companies where climate is genuinely core to the mission

## Quality levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|-------|--------------------|
| **L0 — No structure** | Mission is described in marketing but has no formal structure or accountability |
| **L1 — Decision made** | A conscious decision made about whether and which structure to pursue; reasons documented |
| **L2 — Structure in place** | Relevant legal structure or certification initiated; governance obligations understood and resourced |
| **L3 — Active governance** | Certification current and substantive; reporting obligations met on schedule; mission-related commitments are visible in operating decisions, not just communications |

## How this connects to other goals

- **Founding roles and equity** — PBC conversion or nonprofit arm creation requires board and shareholder approval; the decision about structure should be made before investors are in the cap table, not after
- **Regulatory and industry rules** — nonprofit status creates its own regulatory obligations (state charitable registration, annual filings); [regulatory and industry rules](../regulatory-industry-rules/README.md) is where broader compliance obligations live
- **Board and investor reporting** — if your company has a public benefit or climate reporting obligation, that reporting should be integrated into your investor update cadence, not siloed

{% hint style="success" %}
**Done when:** You have made a documented decision about structure (including the decision to not pursue one), the chosen structure is legally in place or actively being implemented, governance obligations are on the ops calendar, and someone owns them.
{% endhint %}

## Resources

- [B Lab: B Corp certification guide](https://www.bcorporation.net) — the authoritative source for B Corp certification requirements, fees, and the B Impact Assessment; the BIA is free to complete as a self-assessment before committing
- [Delaware PBC resources (BLBC)](https://benefitcorp.net) — the Benefit Corporation Information Center tracks state-level PBC laws and provides plain-English guides to conversion
- [Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)](https://sciencebasedtargets.org) — the standard for credible corporate climate commitments; free guidance on target-setting methodology
- [IRS: Applying for 501(c)(3) status](https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits) — the authoritative source for US nonprofit status; Form 1023-EZ is available for organisations with projected revenue under $50,000
